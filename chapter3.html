# Chapter Three # The Throne That Was Never Ours The longer I studied the story of Adam and Eve, the more I became convinced that the garden reveals far more than the entrance of sin into the world. It reveals something about the human heart that continues to surface in every generation. Adam and Eve were not simply hiding from God among the trees. They were struggling with a new reality they had never known before. For the first time, they stood before truth while carrying guilt, and their response would become a pattern repeated throughout human history. When God spoke to Adam, Adam answered. When God questioned Eve, Eve answered. Yet both responses carried a familiar tension. Neither denied what had happened, but neither accepted responsibility without qualification. Their words moved outward almost as quickly as they moved inward. Attention shifted toward another person, another influence, another cause. As I reflected on those moments, a question emerged that I could not ignore. Why are human beings so quick to evaluate others while remaining reluctant to examine themselves? The question reaches far beyond the boundaries of the garden. It follows us into families, workplaces, churches, and communities. It appears whenever conversations become focused on the failures of others while remaining strangely silent about the condition of our own hearts. It emerges whenever criticism feels easier than repentance or whenever another person's shortcomings become more interesting than our own spiritual growth. At first glance, this may appear unrelated to judgment, but the connection is deeper than it seems. Whenever we focus primarily on the actions of others, we are often doing more than making observations. We are placing ourselves in a position from which we can evaluate, assess, and pronounce conclusions. Sometimes those conclusions are accurate. Sometimes they are not. Either way, something significant is taking place beneath the surface. We are assuming a level of authority that deserves closer examination. The Bible speaks frequently about God's role as Judge. This is not a minor theme hidden in a handful of passages. It appears throughout Scripture because judgment is inseparable from God's sovereignty. He alone possesses complete knowledge. He alone sees the hidden motives that lie beneath outward behavior. He alone understands every circumstance, every struggle, every wound, and every intention that remains invisible to human eyes. That reality should create a sense of humility within all of us. Most of the conflicts that shape our lives are viewed through limited perspectives. We see fragments of stories and assume we possess the whole narrative. We witness actions without understanding the private battles that may have influenced them. We hear words but remain unaware of the fears, disappointments, or wounds from which those words emerged. Human beings often make confident conclusions while working with incomplete information. Scripture repeatedly reminds us that God does not share this limitation. When the prophet Samuel arrived at Jesse's house to anoint the future king of Israel, he immediately noticed the outward appearance of Jesse's sons. One of them seemed like the obvious choice. His stature, appearance, and presence appeared kingly. Samuel's conclusion seemed reasonable until God interrupted it. The Lord reminded Samuel that human beings naturally focus on outward appearances while God looks upon the heart. That statement has challenged believers for generations because it confronts one of our deepest weaknesses. We naturally place great confidence in what we can see. We evaluate actions, appearances, reputations, and impressions. God sees beyond all of them. This does not mean that discernment is unnecessary. In fact, Scripture repeatedly encourages discernment. Believers are called to exercise wisdom, recognize truth, and distinguish between what is holy and what is harmful. Yet discernment and judgment are not identical. Discernment seeks understanding. Judgment seeks final authority. Discernment acknowledges its limitations. Judgment often forgets them. The difference becomes clearer when we look at the ministry of Jesus. Throughout the Gospels, people frequently approached others with conclusions already formed. Religious leaders often seemed certain about who was acceptable and who was not. Categories were established long before conversations began. Certain people were viewed as righteous, while others were viewed as hopeless. Certain individuals deserved acceptance, while others deserved rejection. Then Jesus entered those situations and disrupted expectations. The tax collector was no longer simply a tax collector. The Samaritan woman was no longer simply a Samaritan woman. The blind beggar was no longer simply a blind beggar. Again and again, Jesus looked beyond the labels that others considered sufficient. He saw human beings where others saw categories. He saw stories where others saw reputations. He saw possibilities where others saw conclusions. What strikes me most is that Jesus never ignored truth in order to accomplish this. He never pretended that sin was acceptable or that righteousness was unimportant. Yet His approach to people revealed a depth of understanding that exposed the limitations of human judgment. People are rarely as simple as the labels assigned to them. Every person carries a history that cannot be fully observed from the outside. Every person bears wounds, experiences, victories, failures, fears, and hopes that remain largely hidden from public view. The God who created humanity sees every part of that story simultaneously. We do not. That distinction matters. The more aware we become of our limitations, the more carefully we approach conclusions about others. Humility grows when we recognize how much remains unseen. Pride grows when we assume our perspective is complete. Perhaps this explains why grace and humility often travel together. A person who remembers his own need for mercy usually approaches others differently. The memory of personal failure creates compassion. The memory of forgiveness softens harshness. The awareness that God has patiently worked within our own lives makes it difficult to dismiss the possibility that He may be working within someone else's life as well. As these thoughts settled into my heart, I began to understand why the question at the center of this book is so important. Who made you judge? It is not merely a challenge directed toward outward behavior. It is an invitation to examine the assumptions that quietly shape the way we view others. It asks whether we have mistaken observation for authority. It asks whether we have confused discernment with ownership of a throne that belongs to God alone. The truth is that there is already a Judge, and His throne is occupied. The role of the believer has never been to replace Him. Our calling is to walk humbly before Him, to pursue truth with sincerity, and to allow His Word to search our own hearts before we become preoccupied with the condition of everyone else. As we move forward, another question naturally emerges. If judgment belongs to God and humility belongs to the disciple, how should truth function in our lives? Is truth something we wield against others, or is it something that first transforms us? That question will guide us into the next chapter, where we will explore one of the most misunderstood relationships in the Christian life: the relationship between truth and grace.
