Chapter Fifteen

The Question We Must Ask Ourselves

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 15 of 25

Every meaningful journey eventually reaches a place where the focus can no longer remain on everyone else.

For most of our lives, it is easy to direct our attention outward. We evaluate the world around us, form opinions about the choices people make, and develop conclusions about what is right and wrong. In many ways, this comes naturally. Observing others requires far less courage than examining ourselves.

The most important questions are often the ones we ask ourselves.

Yet the longer I have studied Scripture, the more convinced I have become that God is far more interested in the condition of my heart than in my opinions about someone else's.

This truth appears repeatedly throughout the Bible.

When David stood before Goliath, the story was not merely about a giant. It was about faith. When Elijah stood upon Mount Carmel, the story was not merely about false prophets. It was about obedience. When Jonah ran from Nineveh, the story was not merely about a rebellious city. It was about a reluctant prophet whose heart still needed transformation.

Again and again, God addressed circumstances while simultaneously dealing with the people involved in those circumstances.

The same principle remains true today.

Life presents countless opportunities to focus on the failures of others. Every news headline, every disagreement, every conflict, and every disappointment invites us to become commentators on the shortcomings of those around us. Yet while we are busy analyzing everyone else, a quieter question often waits beneath the surface.

What is God trying to teach me?

That question is rarely as exciting as discussing the mistakes of others, but it is far more important.

Spiritual maturity begins when believers stop viewing every situation primarily through the lens of what others should do and begin asking what God is doing within their own hearts. This shift does not happen automatically. It requires humility. It requires honesty. Most importantly, it requires a willingness to allow truth to become personal.

The disciples experienced this lesson repeatedly.

One of the most striking examples occurs after the resurrection when Peter begins asking questions about another disciple's future. Jesus responds with words that remain relevant for every generation.

"What is that to thee? Follow thou me."

The response is both simple and profound.

Peter's attention had drifted toward someone else's calling, someone else's future, and someone else's relationship with God. Jesus gently redirected him back to the only responsibility he could truly control.

Follow Me.

There is wisdom in that instruction that extends far beyond the immediate conversation.

Many believers spend enormous amounts of energy worrying about what God should do in the lives of others while neglecting what He is already doing in their own. They become experts on external issues while remaining unfamiliar with the deeper work of personal transformation.

Jesus consistently brought people back to themselves.

Not in a selfish sense, but in a spiritual one.

Before God changes our circumstances, He often changes our hearts.

He wanted them to recognize that following Him required continual self examination. It required honesty about motives, attitudes, fears, ambitions, and desires. It required surrender.

That is not always comfortable.

There are moments when it feels easier to carry stones than to carry a cross.

A stone allows us to focus on someone else's shortcomings.

A cross requires us to confront our own.

A stone feeds pride.

A cross produces humility.

A stone creates distance.

A cross draws us closer to God.

Perhaps this explains why Jesus repeatedly called His followers to deny themselves. The greatest obstacle to spiritual growth is often not a lack of information. It is the unwillingness to allow truth to penetrate deeply enough to challenge the parts of us that still resist God's work.

As these reflections continued to unfold in my mind, I found myself returning to a question that appears throughout Scripture in various forms.

Not, "What is wrong with them?"

But, "What is happening within me?"

The difference between those questions is profound.

One keeps the focus outward.

The other invites transformation.

One often leads toward criticism.

The other often leads toward repentance.

One seeks a verdict.

The other seeks growth.

This is where the message of the book becomes intensely personal.

It is possible to agree with every chapter and still miss the point. A person can acknowledge that judgment belongs to God, affirm the importance of grace, appreciate the example of Jesus, and yet never allow those truths to reshape the way he lives.

The purpose of truth has never been mere agreement.

Its purpose is transformation.

Truth was never meant to stop at the mind. It was meant to change the heart.

God does not reveal Himself simply to increase our knowledge. He reveals Himself so that we might become more like Christ.

That process continues throughout the Christian life.

There is always another area to surrender.

There is always another lesson to learn.

There is always another opportunity to grow in humility, wisdom, patience, and love.

The good news is that God remains committed to that process even when we are imperfect participants.

He is patient.

He is faithful.

He is not discouraged by the pace of our growth.

The same grace that saved us continues shaping us.

That realization brings tremendous freedom because it shifts the focus away from performance and back toward relationship. Spiritual growth becomes less about proving ourselves and more about walking with God. The Christian life becomes less about maintaining appearances and more about allowing Christ to form His character within us.

Perhaps that is the question every reader must eventually answer.

Am I more interested in identifying the failures of others, or am I willing to allow God to continue transforming me?

The answer may determine whether this book remains an interesting collection of ideas or becomes part of a much deeper work that the Holy Spirit desires to accomplish.

For the journey toward Christlikeness has always begun in the same place.

Not with a stone in the hand.

But with a heart that is willing to be changed.

End of Chapter Fifteen
