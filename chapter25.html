Chapter Twenty Five

Empty Hands

Every journey must eventually come to an end.

When this book began, it began with a question. That question led us through gardens and wildernesses, through temple courts and empty tombs, through stories of failure and stories of redemption. Along the way we encountered people who hid from God, people who blamed others, people who carried stones, and people who desperately needed mercy. We examined truth, judgment, grace, conviction, condemnation, humility, and redemption. Yet as I reflect upon everything we have explored together, I find myself returning to a much simpler image than any of the others.

I see empty hands.

There was a time when those hands held stones.

They held opinions formed without understanding. They held assumptions built upon incomplete information. They held conclusions reached too quickly and judgments spoken too confidently. At times they carried pride disguised as conviction and certainty disguised as wisdom. Like so many others, I often believed I understood far more than I actually did.

The longer I have walked with God, however, the more I have come to appreciate the beauty of empty hands.

Empty hands are not a sign of weakness. They are evidence of surrender. They reveal a heart that no longer feels compelled to occupy a throne that belongs to God alone. They acknowledge that ultimate judgment rests in His hands, not ours. They recognize that while truth is essential, humility is equally necessary.

Perhaps this is one of the final lessons every believer must learn. Spiritual maturity is not measured by how many stones we can throw or how many arguments we can win. It is not demonstrated by our ability to expose the failures of others or identify every weakness we encounter. Genuine maturity is revealed through a growing awareness of our dependence upon the grace of God.

That awareness changes the way we see everything.

It changes the way we read Scripture because we begin recognizing ourselves within its pages. We see our own tendency to hide when truth becomes uncomfortable. We see our own attempts to justify ourselves when confronted with failure. We see our own need for mercy reflected in the lives of those who encountered Jesus.

It also changes the way we view other people.

When we remember how patient God has been with us, patience becomes easier to extend. When we remember the mercy we have received, mercy becomes easier to offer. When we remember how often God continued writing beyond our failures, we become less likely to assume another person's story has already reached its conclusion.

This perspective does not weaken truth. It deepens our appreciation for it. The truth revealed in Scripture continually reminds us that every person stands on equal ground before God. No one arrives through personal achievement. No one earns redemption through superior morality. Every believer enters the story the same way, through grace.

The cross remains the ultimate reminder of that reality.

At Calvary, the One who possessed every right to judge chose instead to bear judgment. The One who knew every failure carried every burden. The One who could have condemned extended mercy. The Gospel is not the story of humanity climbing toward God. It is the story of God reaching toward humanity.

That truth leaves very little room for pride.

When we stand at the foot of the cross, the distinctions that often seem so important begin to fade. Titles lose their significance. Accomplishments lose their ability to impress. Comparisons lose their power. What remains is the overwhelming realization that we have been loved far more deeply than we deserved.

Gratitude grows in that place.

Humility grows there as well.

The believer who remains near the cross eventually discovers that empty hands are far more valuable than stones. Stones create distance. Empty hands create space for grace. Stones encourage condemnation. Empty hands remain open to mercy. Stones seek control. Empty hands trust God.

As this final chapter comes to a close, my prayer is not that readers remember every argument presented throughout these pages. My prayer is that they remember the Savior who stood at the center of every chapter. He is the One who called Adam from hiding. He is the One who restored Peter after failure. He is the One who defended the woman standing before her accusers. He is the One who carried the cross and rose again in victory.

He is also the One who continues calling people today.

Calling them out of shame.

Calling them out of pride.

Calling them out of condemnation.

Calling them into truth.

Calling them into grace.

Calling them into a life shaped by His character.

The question that began this book has now reached its conclusion.

Who made you judge?

No one did.

The throne belongs to God.

The judgment belongs to God.

The final verdict belongs to God.

Our calling is simpler and far more beautiful.

We are called to follow Jesus.

We are called to love truth.

We are called to extend mercy.

We are called to walk humbly.

And when the journey is over, may we be found standing before Him with nothing in our hands but gratitude for the grace that carried us home.
