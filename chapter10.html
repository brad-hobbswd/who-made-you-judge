Chapter Ten

A Church Without Stones

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 10 of 25

The cross changes more than a person's understanding of salvation. It changes the way that person sees everyone else.

This truth is easy to affirm in theory and much more difficult to practice in daily life. Most believers readily agree that they have been forgiven through grace. They understand that Christ bore what they could never carry and paid a debt they could never repay. Yet even after embracing these truths for themselves, many continue to interact with others as though grace should be measured, rationed, or earned.

The cross changes not only where we are going. It changes how we treat the people walking beside us.

The tension is not new.

From the earliest days of the church, believers have wrestled with the challenge of extending to others the same mercy they themselves have received. Human nature seems far more comfortable receiving grace than giving it. We celebrate forgiveness when it reaches our lives, yet we often struggle when forgiveness is needed by someone who has disappointed, offended, or wounded us.

Perhaps that is why the teachings of Jesus repeatedly return to the subject of mercy.

He understood something that remains true today. A person who forgets the depth of his own forgiveness will eventually become harsh toward the failures of others. When grace fades from memory, judgment quickly fills the space it leaves behind.

The church was never intended to become a gathering place for people who believed they had achieved spiritual superiority. From its very beginning, it was a community of redeemed people learning to walk together in the grace of God. Every congregation contains individuals at different stages of growth. Some have followed Christ for decades. Others have only recently begun their journey. Some carry visible struggles. Others battle private burdens that remain unseen by everyone around them.

The common bond is not perfection.

It is redemption.

This reality becomes especially important whenever sin, failure, or weakness appears within the life of a believer. Communities often reveal their true character in the way they respond to those moments. Some environments react primarily with suspicion and distance. Others respond as though truth no longer matters. Neither approach reflects the pattern established by Christ.

Throughout His ministry, Jesus demonstrated that truth and grace were never meant to compete with one another. He confronted what was wrong while continuing to pursue restoration. He called people to repentance while refusing to abandon them in their brokenness. His goal was never merely to identify problems. His goal was to redeem people.

A church shaped by the cross adopts the same perspective.

Such a church does not celebrate sin, excuse harmful behavior, or abandon biblical convictions. Holiness remains important because the character of God remains important. At the same time, correction is motivated by restoration rather than humiliation. Accountability exists to bring healing rather than shame. Difficult conversations occur within the context of love rather than condemnation.

This distinction changes everything.

When people believe they will be condemned, they often become skilled at hiding. They learn how to protect their image, conceal their struggles, and present a version of themselves that appears stronger than reality. The result is a community filled with appearances but lacking honesty.

Grace creates a different environment.

When believers understand that truth will be met with compassion, they become more willing to step into the light. Confession becomes possible. Healing becomes possible. Growth becomes possible. People no longer spend their energy maintaining an illusion of perfection because they know that their acceptance rests in Christ rather than in their performance.

Grace creates the kind of community where honesty is no longer feared.

This does not mean every situation is simple.

Churches must still address conflict. Leaders must still exercise wisdom. Sin must still be confronted when necessary. Scripture never suggests otherwise. The difference lies in the spirit with which these responsibilities are carried out.

There is a profound difference between helping someone carry a burden and using that burden as evidence of personal superiority.

One reflects the heart of Christ.

The other reflects the instincts of fallen humanity.

As I considered the life of the early church, I found myself drawn to the countless examples of imperfect people whom God continued to use. Peter struggled and grew. John matured over time. Thomas wrestled with doubt. Mark experienced failure before becoming a valuable servant in ministry. The New Testament is filled with people whose stories remind us that spiritual growth is often a process rather than a single moment.

God did not discard these individuals because of their weaknesses.

He worked within their weaknesses.

That observation should influence the way believers view one another today.

Every person sitting in a church sanctuary is a work in progress. Some changes occur quickly. Others require years of growth, prayer, and discipleship. The God who patiently works within our lives often asks us to extend that same patience to others.

The alternative is a culture in which people become more concerned with protecting appearances than pursuing transformation. Such environments may appear strong from the outside, but they often leave little room for honesty. The fear of judgment becomes greater than the desire for healing.

The ministry of Jesus consistently moved in the opposite direction.

People who knew they needed help were drawn to Him.

People burdened by failure found hope in His presence.

People who had been defined by their past discovered that redemption could reshape their future.

The church is called to reflect that same reality.

When visitors walk through the doors, they should encounter truth. They should also encounter grace. When believers stumble, they should be called toward repentance. They should also find support as they pursue restoration. When difficult situations arise, biblical convictions should remain firm, but compassion should remain equally visible.

A church without stones is not a church without truth. It is a church that remembers the cross.

A church without stones is not a church without standards.

It is a church that remembers who carried the cross.

It is a church that understands the difference between restoration and condemnation.

It is a church that recognizes that every person standing in need of grace today is standing exactly where someone else once stood.

Most importantly, it is a church that understands its own story. The community of faith exists because mercy triumphed where judgment could have prevailed. Every testimony, every transformed life, and every redeemed heart points back to that reality.

As we continue our journey, the focus becomes even more personal. Understanding how a church should respond is important, but each believer must eventually answer a more individual question. If grace and truth are meant to shape our relationships, how do we speak truth in a way that reflects the character of Jesus? How do we address difficult issues without slipping into the patterns of accusation and condemnation that have followed humanity since the garden?

The answer requires us to look not only at what Jesus taught, but also at how He communicated with the people He came to save.
