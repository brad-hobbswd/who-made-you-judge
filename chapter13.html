Chapter Thirteen

Truth Is a Person

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 13 of 25

When this journey began, it began with a question.

Questions have a way of inviting us into places we might never have entered otherwise. They cause us to slow down, reconsider assumptions, and examine beliefs that may have remained unchallenged for years. The question that guided these chapters was simple enough to be asked in a single sentence, yet profound enough to require an entire book.

Every question in this journey has ultimately pointed to one Person.

Who made you judge?

Along the way, we discovered that the answer was not found in human opinion, religious tradition, or personal experience. The answer was found in Scripture. We followed the story from the garden to the cross. We watched humanity hide, shift blame, and struggle beneath the weight of sin. We examined the difference between conviction and condemnation, explored the contrast between the accuser and the Advocate, and observed the way Jesus interacted with people whose lives were marked by failure, shame, and brokenness.

Yet beneath every chapter, another truth has quietly been unfolding.

This book was never ultimately about judgment.

It was always about Jesus.

The danger of discussing truth, grace, mercy, and judgment is that these words can slowly become abstract ideas. We can spend so much time defining them that we forget they are most clearly understood through a person. Christianity is not built upon concepts alone. It is built upon the revelation of God manifested in flesh.

That reality changes everything.

Truth is not merely a principle to defend.

Truth is a person to know.

Truth walked the roads of Galilee.

Truth sat beside wells and spoke with outcasts.

Truth touched lepers, welcomed children, restored failures, and wept at the tomb of a friend.

Truth entered a world filled with darkness and refused to be overcome by it.

When Jesus declared, "I am the way, the truth, and the life," He was making a statement that reaches far beyond doctrine. He was revealing that truth cannot be separated from His character. Any understanding of truth that does not resemble Christ has already begun to drift from its source.

This realization has profound implications.

It means that truth cannot delight in cruelty because Jesus did not delight in cruelty.

It means that truth cannot celebrate condemnation because Jesus did not celebrate condemnation.

It means that truth cannot exist apart from love because Jesus demonstrated love everywhere He went.

The more closely we examine His life, the more difficult it becomes to justify attitudes that contradict His example.

Jesus possessed perfect knowledge, yet He remained patient.

He possessed absolute authority, yet He remained humble.

He possessed every right to judge, yet He continually extended mercy.

These realities do not weaken truth.

They reveal its true nature.

Truth is not merely spoken. Truth is lived. Truth is Jesus.

For many years, I assumed that truth was primarily something to be proclaimed. While that is certainly part of the Christian calling, I have come to realize that truth must first be encountered. Before it can be spoken effectively, it must shape the heart of the one speaking it.

The people most transformed by Jesus were not those who merely heard His words.

They were those who encountered His presence.

Peter was changed because he encountered Jesus.

Zacchaeus was changed because he encountered Jesus.

The Samaritan woman was changed because she encountered Jesus.

The woman caught in adultery was changed because she encountered Jesus.

Everything ultimately comes back to Him.

That observation brings us to the conclusion of this book.

The goal was never to convince readers to lower biblical standards. It was never to suggest that sin is unimportant or that truth should be ignored. Rather, the goal has been to encourage a return to the example of Christ Himself. He remains the clearest picture of what truth looks like when it is expressed perfectly.

When believers lose sight of Jesus, truth can become harsh.

When believers lose sight of Jesus, grace can become shallow.

When believers lose sight of Jesus, judgment can become distorted.

But when Jesus remains at the center, everything begins to find its proper place.

Truth remains firm without becoming cruel.

Grace remains abundant without becoming permissive.

Mercy remains available without compromising holiness.

The Christian life is not about choosing between truth and grace. It is about remaining close to the One in whom both are perfectly united.

Perhaps that is why the Gospel continues to transform lives after all these centuries. It does not merely present a set of teachings. It presents a Savior. It does not merely offer information. It offers relationship. It does not simply expose what is wrong. It reveals the One who can make all things new.

Christianity is not merely about believing truth. It is about following Jesus.

As these pages come to an end, my prayer is not that readers remember every argument or every observation. My prayer is that they remember Jesus.

When confronted with failure, may they remember Jesus.

When tempted to condemn, may they remember Jesus.

When struggling to extend grace, may they remember Jesus.

When seeking truth, may they remember Jesus.

For in remembering Him, we discover that truth is not a weapon to wield against others. It is a light that leads us to the Savior. It is a mirror that reveals our need for Him. It is a pathway that continually draws us closer to the heart of God.

The question that opened this book has now been answered.

The throne belongs to God.

The judgment belongs to God.

The glory belongs to God.

And we, the recipients of extraordinary mercy, are invited to walk humbly before Him, reflecting the character of the One who loved us enough to bear our judgment and call us His own.

In the end, truth is not merely something we believe.

Truth is Someone we follow.

End of Chapter Thirteen
