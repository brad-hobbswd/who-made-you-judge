# Chapter Eight # Seeing People the Way Jesus Did The encounter in the temple courts leaves us with an important question. If the story of the woman caught in adultery reveals the heart of Jesus in a moment of public accusation, was that response unique to that particular situation, or was it part of a larger pattern that defined His entire ministry? The answer becomes clear as we continue reading the Gospels. The compassion Jesus demonstrated that day was not an isolated event. It was the consistent expression of how He interacted with people throughout His earthly ministry. Whether He was speaking with fishermen, tax collectors, religious leaders, widows, lepers, or those society had pushed to the margins, He repeatedly revealed an ability to see beyond the labels that others assigned. This does not mean He ignored sin or overlooked truth. In fact, few people spoke more honestly about the condition of the human heart than Jesus. He addressed hypocrisy when He encountered it. He confronted pride when it disguised itself as righteousness. He challenged unbelief, exposed self deception, and called people to repentance. Yet even while doing so, He approached individuals in a way that preserved their humanity. He never reduced people to a single moment in their lives. That distinction is worth considering because human beings often do the opposite. We tend to remember people according to the most visible part of their story. A failure becomes their identity. A mistake becomes their reputation. A weakness becomes the lens through which everything else is viewed. Jesus consistently resisted that tendency. When He encountered Simon Peter, He saw more than an impulsive fisherman struggling to understand what discipleship would require. He saw a man who would one day strengthen others through the lessons learned from his own failures. Even after Peter denied Him, Jesus did not abandon the future He had already seen. When He encountered Zacchaeus, many people saw a dishonest tax collector whose profession made him an object of suspicion and resentment. Jesus saw a man whose heart was ready for transformation. Before Zacchaeus ever demonstrated change, Jesus invited Himself into his home. The invitation did not ignore the man's past. It created the environment in which repentance could occur. The order is significant. Transformation followed the encounter. It did not precede it. The same pattern appears in the life of the Samaritan woman. When she arrived at the well, she carried a complicated history that would have caused many people to keep their distance. Cultural barriers alone should have prevented the conversation from taking place. Religious customs discouraged it. Social expectations discouraged it. Public opinion discouraged it. Jesus spoke to her anyway. As their conversation unfolded, He addressed truths about her life that could not be avoided. Yet every revelation moved the discussion toward hope rather than humiliation. By the time the encounter ended, the woman who came searching for water had become one of the first people to carry the message of the Messiah back to her community. What becomes increasingly evident throughout these accounts is that Jesus never viewed people as finished stories. Where others saw conclusions, He saw possibilities. Where others saw failure, He saw potential redemption. Where others saw reasons to withdraw, He saw opportunities to restore. Perhaps this is one of the reasons the ministry of Jesus continues to challenge believers today. His example forces us to examine the way we look at people. It is possible to be correct about a person's struggles while being completely wrong about how to respond to them. It is possible to recognize sin while forgetting the purpose of redemption. It is possible to speak truth while failing to reflect the character of the One who is Truth. The Gospels repeatedly remind us that Jesus did more than identify problems. He entered people's stories. He listened, engaged, taught, challenged, and restored. His goal was never merely to expose what was broken. His goal was to bring healing to what had been broken. This becomes especially important when we remember the central question of this book. Who made you judge? The question is not asking believers to abandon discernment. Neither is it suggesting that truth no longer matters. Instead, it asks whether our view of others has been shaped more by human instincts than by the example of Christ. Judgment often begins with a conclusion. Jesus often began with a conversation. Judgment tends to focus on what a person has done. Jesus consistently looked toward what a person could become through the work of God. The difference may seem subtle at first, but its impact is profound. One approach creates distance. The other creates opportunity for transformation. As I reflected on these encounters, I found myself wondering how differently churches might function if believers embraced the perspective demonstrated by Jesus. What would happen if we became as committed to restoration as we are to correction? What would happen if we viewed every struggling person as someone whose story was still being written by God? The question is not theoretical. Every congregation contains people carrying wounds that are invisible to everyone around them. Every community contains individuals whose greatest failures are known publicly while their deepest battles remain hidden. Every believer has experienced moments when grace accomplished what criticism never could. The ministry of Jesus reminds us that redemption is rarely instantaneous. God often works through conversations, relationships, seasons of growth, and moments of conviction that unfold over time. The people who encountered Christ did not emerge from those meetings as perfect individuals. They emerged as people moving toward transformation. That distinction matters because it reflects the patience of God. The Lord who called fishermen, restored failures, welcomed outcasts, and transformed sinners continues to work in the same way today. His commitment to truth has never changed, but neither has His commitment to redemption. As we move forward, another aspect of this journey demands our attention. If Jesus consistently viewed people through the lens of redemption, then the cross becomes the ultimate expression of that perspective. Every conversation, every miracle, every act of compassion, and every call to repentance ultimately points toward a single event where truth, justice, mercy, and grace converge. To understand judgment rightly, we must eventually stand at the foot of the cross and consider what happened there. Only then can we fully appreciate the difference between a God who delights in condemnation and a Savior who willingly bore judgment Himself so that others might live.
