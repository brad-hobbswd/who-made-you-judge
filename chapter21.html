Chapter Twenty One

When You Are the One Holding the Stone

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 21 of 25

One of the most uncomfortable discoveries in the Christian life is realizing that the person we have been reading about throughout a book is sometimes ourselves.

The hardest mirror
to face
is the one
that reveals
our own heart.

It is easy to recognize the Pharisees when they appear on the pages of Scripture. Their mistakes seem obvious from a distance. We read their conversations with Jesus and wonder how they could have missed what stood directly in front of them. We see their harshness, their pride, and their certainty, and we assume we would have responded differently.

Yet Scripture has a remarkable way of turning the mirror toward the reader.

The longer I studied the accounts we have explored throughout this journey, the more I realized that the greatest danger is not becoming the woman standing in need of mercy. Most believers readily acknowledge that need. The greater danger is becoming so familiar with grace that we forget how desperately we still depend upon it.

That forgetfulness often happens gradually.

No one wakes up intending to become judgmental.

No one decides to become self righteous.

The process is usually much quieter than that.

It begins when convictions slowly become a source of pride rather than gratitude.

It begins when spiritual growth becomes a reason to compare ourselves with others rather than a reason to thank God for His patience.

It begins when we spend more time evaluating the condition of other hearts than examining our own.

The change is subtle enough that it can remain unnoticed for years.

A person may continue attending church, reading Scripture, praying faithfully, and serving others while quietly developing an attitude that stands in direct opposition to the spirit of Christ. The outward activities remain intact, but the heart begins drifting toward superiority.

That is why the image of the stone remains so powerful.

A stone feels justified when it is first picked up.

It feels deserved.

It feels necessary.

The person holding it is often convinced that truth is on his side.

The danger is that stones rarely remain limited to the situations that first inspired them. Over time, they become habits of thought. They shape the way we view people. They influence conversations, assumptions, and expectations. Eventually, they become so familiar that we stop noticing we are carrying them at all.

The religious leaders who brought the woman before Jesus almost certainly believed they were defending righteousness. They did not arrive at the temple thinking of themselves as villains. They believed they were protecting truth. Yet somewhere along the way, they became more interested in exposing sin than participating in redemption.

Truth without
the heart of Christ
can become
a stone
in our hands.

That possibility should cause every believer to pause.

It is possible to defend truth while losing sight of its purpose.

It is possible to speak accurately while speaking without love.

It is possible to become so focused on another person's failure that we forget our own need for grace.

As I reflected on these realities, I found myself asking a question that every follower of Christ must eventually confront.

What stones am I still carrying?

The answer may not involve public condemnation or obvious criticism. Some stones are far more subtle. They appear in assumptions we make about people whose stories we do not fully understand. They appear in the conclusions we reach before listening carefully. They appear in the private satisfaction that occasionally accompanies another person's failure because it makes our own shortcomings seem smaller by comparison.

These moments reveal something important.

The issue is rarely the stone itself.

The issue is what the stone represents.

Every stone reflects a belief that we are qualified to determine more than we actually know. It assumes that our perspective is complete, our understanding is sufficient, and our conclusions are reliable enough to stand where only God can stand.

The Gospel continually calls believers away from that posture.

Not because truth is unimportant, but because humility is essential.

The closer a person draws to Christ, the more aware that person becomes of how much remains unseen. The complexities of human lives become more apparent. The hidden battles people fight become easier to imagine. The need for mercy becomes impossible to ignore because we encounter that need within ourselves every day.

The strongest believer
is not the one
who throws the farthest.
It is the one
who lets go first.

This awareness does not weaken conviction.

It deepens compassion.

A believer can remain firmly committed to truth while recognizing that every person encountered is someone for whom Christ died. Such a perspective changes the tone of conversations, the spirit of correction, and the way disagreements are approached.

Most importantly, it changes the way we view ourselves.

The moment we recognize that we are capable of carrying stones is the moment we become more willing to put them down.

That willingness is not weakness.

It is wisdom.

It is the recognition that the throne belongs to God, the judgment belongs to God, and the work of transforming hearts belongs to God as well.

Our calling is different.

We are called to walk in truth, extend mercy, and remember that every person we encounter is standing in need of the same grace that has carried us this far.

Perhaps the greatest evidence of spiritual maturity is not how many stones a person can throw.

Perhaps it is how many stones a person has learned to release.
