Chapter Eighteen

The Freedom Beyond Being Right

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 18 of 25

As this journey has unfolded, I have found myself returning to a realization that was neither immediate nor comfortable. For much of my life, I assumed that spiritual maturity and spiritual certainty were nearly the same thing. The more answers a person possessed, the more mature that person appeared. The more confidently someone could defend a position, the stronger that individual's faith seemed to be. Without realizing it, I had begun measuring growth by the ability to be right.

The goal of truth is not merely to make us correct. It is to make us like Christ.

There is certainly value in conviction. Scripture never encourages believers to become indifferent toward truth. God has revealed Himself through His Word, and that revelation matters. Truth matters. Doctrine matters. Faithfulness matters. Yet as I continued studying the life of Jesus, I began to notice something that challenged my assumptions.

The people who opposed Him most fiercely were often people who believed they were right.

They knew the Scriptures. They understood the traditions. They could quote passages, explain regulations, and defend their positions with remarkable confidence. Their problem was not a lack of information. Their problem was that information had not produced the transformation God desired.

Somewhere along the way, being correct had become more important than becoming like the God they claimed to serve.

That observation forced me to ask a difficult question.

Is it possible for a person to possess truth and still miss its purpose?

The answer seems unavoidable.

Truth was never given merely to increase our knowledge. It was given to draw us closer to God and to shape us into the image of Christ. When truth accomplishes only the first objective while failing to accomplish the second, something essential has been lost.

This tension appears throughout the Gospels. Jesus repeatedly encountered individuals whose understanding was impressive but whose hearts remained untouched by the deeper work of grace. They could identify error in others with remarkable speed, yet often failed to recognize pride within themselves. They were skilled at evaluating outward behavior while remaining unaware of the condition of their own hearts.

The danger of such a posture remains just as real today.

There is a subtle temptation that accompanies knowledge. The more a person learns, the easier it becomes to assume that understanding alone is evidence of maturity. Yet knowledge and Christlikeness are not identical. One concerns what we know. The other concerns who we are becoming.

The Apostle Paul understood this distinction well. Writing to the Corinthians, he warned that knowledge by itself can produce pride. His concern was not that knowledge was bad. His concern was that knowledge disconnected from love can create an illusion of spiritual growth while leaving the heart unchanged.

Love accomplishes something that information alone never can.

Love keeps truth connected to its purpose.

Love reminds us that every doctrine ultimately points beyond itself to the God who revealed it.

Love protects believers from treating truth as a trophy to display rather than a gift to steward.

Truth without love can win an argument. Truth with love can change a life.

As these thoughts settled into my heart, I began noticing how often the desire to be right influences human relationships. Conversations that should produce understanding can quickly become contests of certainty. Discussions that could encourage growth sometimes become opportunities to establish superiority. People stop listening because they are too busy preparing their next response.

The tragedy is that everyone involved may possess pieces of the truth while simultaneously failing to reflect the spirit of Christ.

Jesus never demonstrated that kind of insecurity.

When people questioned Him, He remained composed.

When others challenged Him, He remained steady.

He was never frantic.

He never seemed desperate to prove Himself.

His confidence rested in His relationship with the Father rather than in the approval of those around Him.

There is tremendous freedom in that example.

The believer who understands this no longer feels responsible for winning every argument or controlling every outcome. Such a person remains committed to truth, but that commitment is no longer driven by fear, pride, or the need for validation. Instead, it is rooted in a desire to honor God.

This changes the way relationships are approached.

Listening becomes more important because understanding matters.

Patience becomes easier because growth is often a process.

Humility begins to replace defensiveness because personal identity is no longer dependent upon always appearing correct.

None of this weakens conviction.

If anything, it strengthens it.

Truth carried by humility often reaches places that force and arrogance never can. A gracious spirit opens doors that harshness leaves closed. A compassionate heart creates opportunities for conversations that might never occur otherwise. People are far more likely to hear truth when they sense genuine love behind it.

This helps explain why so many people were drawn to Jesus. He never compromised truth, yet those burdened by failure continued seeking Him. The broken approached Him. The wounded approached Him. The outcasts approached Him. Something about His character allowed people to encounter truth without feeling reduced to their worst mistakes.

The closer we become to Christ,
the less interested we are in proving ourselves,
and the more interested we are in serving others.

That reality should challenge every believer.

If people consistently experienced both truth and grace in the presence of Jesus, then perhaps they should experience the same combination in the lives of those who follow Him.

Perhaps spiritual maturity involves more than recognizing error.

Perhaps it also involves reflecting Christ.

Perhaps growth involves more than accumulating knowledge.

Perhaps it involves becoming the kind of person through whom truth can be received.

The longer I walk with God, the more convinced I become that the ultimate goal is not simply to be right about Him. The goal is to become more like Him. Knowledge serves that purpose. Doctrine serves that purpose. Truth serves that purpose. Everything points toward transformation.

For what good is it to understand grace if we fail to extend it? What good is it to speak about mercy if we refuse to show it? What good is it to defend truth if our lives do not reflect the One who is Truth?

The freedom beyond being right is not freedom from conviction.

It is freedom from the need to elevate ourselves through conviction.

It is the freedom that comes from remembering mercy.

It is the freedom that comes from walking in humility.

It is the freedom that comes from allowing truth to shape our hearts before we attempt to place it upon the shoulders of others.

And perhaps that is one of the most beautiful discoveries in the Christian life. The closer we draw to Christ, the less interested we become in standing above people and the more interested we become in helping them find the same grace that found us.

End of Chapter Eighteen
