# Chapter Seven # The Woman, the Stones, and the Savior The distinction between the accuser and the Advocate is not merely a theological concept. It appears repeatedly throughout Scripture, often in moments where human lives hang in the balance. One voice seeks condemnation. The other seeks restoration. One is eager to expose guilt and leave it uncovered. The other reveals the truth while creating a path toward redemption. Few passages illustrate this contrast more vividly than the account of the woman caught in adultery. The scene unfolds in the temple courts, a place where people gathered to hear Jesus teach. It begins as an ordinary day, but the atmosphere changes abruptly when a group of religious leaders arrives with a woman in their custody. Their entrance is neither quiet nor subtle. They bring her directly into the center of the gathering and force her to stand before the crowd. The circumstances immediately raise questions. The woman is present, but the man involved is nowhere to be found. The accusation focuses entirely upon her. Whatever concern these men may have claimed for righteousness, their actions suggest that something else is taking place beneath the surface. The situation feels less like a pursuit of justice and more like an attempt to create a public spectacle. The woman stands exposed. The crowd watches. The religious leaders prepare their case. Then they turn their attention toward Jesus. "Master, this woman was taken in adultery, in the very act." Their statement leaves little room for ambiguity. The accusation is direct, and the law regarding adultery is well known. Everything appears straightforward. The evidence seems conclusive. The expectation is clear. Now a decision must be made. Yet beneath their words lies another purpose. John tells us that they were testing Jesus. The woman may have been the visible subject of the encounter, but she was not the only target. The religious leaders hoped to force Jesus into an impossible position. If He ignored the law, they could accuse Him of disregarding Scripture. If He demanded punishment, they could undermine His reputation for mercy. In their minds, the trap was perfect. The remarkable thing is that Jesus refuses to play by their rules. Rather than answering immediately, He bends down and begins writing on the ground. For centuries, readers have wondered what He wrote. Scripture never tells us, and perhaps that omission is intentional. The focus of the story is not the writing itself but what happens while He writes. The tension begins to shift. The men who arrived expecting to control the moment suddenly find themselves waiting. The crowd waits. The woman waits. Even the accusers wait. The silence forces everyone present to remain in the situation longer than they intended. Then Jesus stands and speaks. "He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her." The statement is simple, yet its effect is profound. Notice what Jesus does not say. He does not deny that adultery is wrong. He does not dismiss the seriousness of sin. He does not declare the woman innocent. Instead, He directs attention toward the hearts of those holding the stones. The focus of the story changes immediately. Only moments earlier, every eye had been fixed upon the woman. Now every person present is forced to examine himself. The shift is extraordinary because it transforms the entire encounter. The accusers came prepared to evaluate someone else's life. Jesus invites them to consider their own. This is not an attempt to avoid truth. It is an expansion of truth. The issue is no longer limited to the woman standing before them. The issue now includes every heart in the circle. One by one, the stones begin to fall. John records that the oldest leave first, followed by the others, until no accusers remain. The image is powerful. Men who arrived confident in their position depart in silence. Men who came prepared to pronounce judgment leave without speaking another word. Men who expected to expose another person's failure discover their own need for mercy. The woman remains. Jesus remains. The crowd remains. For the first time, there is no one standing between the accused woman and the Savior. The conversation that follows is among the most beautiful in all of Scripture. "Woman, where are those thine accusers? Hath no man condemned thee?" She answers, "No man, Lord." Then Jesus responds. "Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more." These words are often quoted, but they are rarely considered together. Some focus on the first half of the statement and ignore the second. Others focus on the second half and overlook the first. Jesus does neither. Mercy and truth remain united. He does not condemn her. He also does not leave her unchanged. He offers grace without excusing sin. He acknowledges sin without withholding grace. Everything we explored in previous chapters converges in this moment. The accusers wanted condemnation. The Advocate offered restoration. The accusers focused on a person's failure. The Advocate focused on a person's future. The accusers arrived carrying stones. The Advocate arrived carrying truth. As I reflected on this passage, I found myself considering not only the woman but also the men who surrounded her. Their actions are easy to criticize because they appear so obvious from a distance. Yet the temptation they faced remains remarkably familiar. Most people have never carried literal stones into a public square. Many, however, have carried assumptions. Many have carried conclusions. Many have carried opinions formed without understanding the full story. The stone is not always physical. Sometimes it is a harsh judgment. Sometimes it is a careless label. Sometimes it is a refusal to believe that grace can change a person. The human heart still reaches for stones far more often than we realize. This is why the story remains so relevant. It forces readers to decide where they stand within the scene. Most people initially identify with the woman because everyone understands the need for mercy. Yet the longer we remain in the passage, the more uncomfortable another possibility becomes. What if we have also stood among those holding stones? What if there have been moments when we became more interested in exposing failure than participating in restoration? What if we have mistaken accusation for righteousness? The questions are uncomfortable, but they are necessary because the story is not merely about one woman and one crowd. It is about every human heart that has ever needed grace. That realization brings us back to the central question of this book. Who made you judge? The men in this story assumed a role that belonged to God. They approached another person's life with certainty while remaining largely unaware of their own need for mercy. Jesus dismantled that illusion with a single sentence. The One person present who possessed the authority to condemn chose instead to extend grace. That truth should leave a lasting impression upon every believer. If the sinless Savior responded this way, what should characterize those who have themselves received mercy? The answer leads naturally into the next stage of our journey. The story of the woman caught in adultery reveals how Jesus responded when confronted with human failure. The next question is equally important. How did Jesus consistently interact with broken people throughout His ministry, and what does that teach us about the relationship between truth, grace, and transformation?
