Chapter Twenty Four

Who Made You Judge?

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 24 of 25

Every journey has a destination.

Some destinations are places. Others are realizations. As I reflect upon everything explored throughout these pages, it becomes clear that the question that inspired this book was never intended to produce a simple answer. It was intended to produce a deeper understanding of God, ourselves, and the way we relate to one another.

Who made you judge?

The throne
was never ours.
It always belonged
to God.

When the question was first asked, it seemed directed toward outward behavior. It appeared to challenge criticism, condemnation, and the tendency to point fingers. While it certainly addresses those things, the journey has revealed that the issue runs much deeper.

The question ultimately exposes a struggle that has existed since the beginning.

It appeared in the garden when blame replaced responsibility.

It appeared throughout Israel's history whenever people elevated themselves above others.

It appeared in the religious leaders who carried stones into the temple courtyard.

And it continues to appear whenever believers forget the grace that has carried them.

At its heart, judgment is often an attempt to occupy a position that was never entrusted to humanity.

The desire itself is understandable.

People want certainty.

People want clear answers.

People want to determine who is right and who is wrong, who deserves mercy and who deserves accountability, who belongs and who does not.

The difficulty is that such authority requires knowledge no human being possesses.

Only God sees every motive.

Only God understands every wound.

Only God knows every circumstance, every struggle, every fear, and every hidden burden that influences the human heart.

The throne was never empty.

It was never waiting for us.

It already belongs to Him.

This realization changes the way Scripture is read.

The stories we have explored throughout this book begin to fit together like pieces of a larger picture. Adam and Eve reveal humanity's tendency to avoid personal responsibility. The woman caught in adultery reveals the danger of carrying stones. Peter reveals the power of restoration. The cross reveals the cost of redemption. The life of Jesus reveals what truth looks like when it is perfectly united with grace.

Together, they tell a single story.

It is the story of a God who continually moves toward people rather than away from them.

A God who reveals truth not to destroy but to redeem.

A God who understands justice more deeply than we ever could and yet remains rich in mercy.

Humility
is not the absence
of truth.
It is truth
carried by grace.

The longer I reflected upon this reality, the more I realized that the opposite of judgment is not indifference.

The opposite of judgment is humility.

Humility does not deny truth.

Humility does not excuse sin.

Humility simply recognizes that every person stands in need of grace and that none of us occupy the throne.

That posture changes everything.

When humility is present, conversations become gentler.

Correction becomes more compassionate.

Relationships become healthier.

The church becomes a place where transformation can flourish rather than a place where appearances must be protected.

Most importantly, humility keeps believers close to the heart of Christ.

Jesus possessed every right to judge.

He possessed complete knowledge.

He possessed perfect holiness.

He possessed absolute authority.

Yet His life was marked by mercy, patience, compassion, and redemption.

If the One qualified to condemn chose instead to extend grace, then those who follow Him must take that example seriously.

As this journey draws near its conclusion, I find myself returning to the image of the temple courtyard.

The stones have fallen.

The crowd has dispersed.

The accusations have faded into silence.

The Gospel
does not invite us
to carry stones.
It invites us
to follow Christ.

What remains is a Savior standing before a sinner and offering the possibility of a different future.

That image captures the heart of the Gospel.

God is not looking for opportunities to condemn.

He is making opportunities for redemption.

He is not searching for reasons to reject people.

He is extending invitations to return.

He is not asking believers to occupy His throne.

He is asking them to follow His Son.

Perhaps that is the answer we have been seeking all along.

Who made you judge?

No one did.

The throne belongs to God.

The judgment belongs to God.

The final verdict belongs to God.

Our calling is something altogether different.

We are called to walk humbly, love mercy, pursue truth, and reflect the character of Jesus Christ in a world that desperately needs Him.

And when we finally understand that, we discover a freedom that judgment could never provide.

End of Chapter Twenty Four
