Chapter One What If We Have Misunderstood Judgment? The conversation began unexpectedly. I was reading familiar passages that I had encountered countless times before when a question quietly settled into my spirit and refused to leave. The words on the page had not changed, yet the assumptions I carried into those words suddenly demanded examination. I realized that I had spent years hearing the word judgment without ever asking whether I understood it as Scripture intended or merely as tradition had presented it. That realization may not seem significant at first, but it changes the way a person reads the Bible. For many believers, judgment is associated with criticism, public correction, and the exposure of another person’s failures. It is the image of a finger pointed across a room or a voice announcing guilt with certainty and finality. It has become so closely connected to truth that many people assume the two words are interchangeable. If truth is spoken, they believe judgment has occurred. If judgment is avoided, they fear truth has been compromised. The more I searched the Scriptures, however, the more difficult that assumption became to maintain. “May I ask you a question?” the author in me asked. The reader answered, “Of course.” “When you hear the word judgment, what do you picture?” “I picture someone deciding whether another person is right or wrong.” “Then let me ask another question. When God first encountered humanity after sin entered the world, what did He actually do?” The reader paused. “He judged them.” “Did He?” Silence has a way of inviting discovery that arguments never can. The book of Genesis records that Adam and Eve had eaten from the tree that God commanded them to avoid. They immediately recognized their nakedness, covered themselves with fig leaves, and hid among the trees of the garden. Shame entered the human story, and fear followed close behind. Everything about the moment suggests that judgment should have arrived without delay. Yet the first recorded words spoken by God after humanity’s rebellion were remarkably simple. “Where are you?” The question deserves careful attention because it reveals something profound about the heart of God. The Creator of heaven and earth was not searching for information. The One who formed Adam from the dust had not suddenly lost sight of him among the trees. The question was never about location. It was about relationship. God invited Adam to step out of hiding before He ever addressed the consequences of sin. That observation changes the entire atmosphere of the narrative. Instead of rushing toward condemnation, God begins with invitation. Instead of exposing Adam before creation, He calls him into conversation. The first movement of God is not toward destruction but toward truth. The reader interrupts. “Are you saying there was no judgment in the garden?” “Not at all. There were consequences, and those consequences were real. The ground would resist man’s labor, pain would enter childbirth, and death would become the destiny of every human body. Judgment was neither ignored nor diminished.” “Then what is different?” “The order is different. Truth comes first. God calls, God speaks, and God reveals before judgment is pronounced.” Perhaps that order has been overlooked because we often approach Scripture carrying assumptions formed by experience rather than by careful observation. We have become accustomed to believing that God’s first response to failure is anger when the biblical record consistently reveals His desire for repentance. The prophets did not appear without warning. Noah preached before the flood. Jonah preached before Nineveh faced destruction. John the Baptist prepared the way before Christ began His ministry. Again and again, truth arrived as an invitation to return. This pattern reaches its fullest expression in Jesus Christ. He walked among people whose lives were marked by brokenness, failure, and sin, yet they continually moved toward Him rather than away from Him. Tax collectors shared meals with Him. Fishermen left their nets to follow Him. Women who carried the weight of shame discovered dignity in His presence. Even those whose lives had been defined by poor choices found themselves strangely drawn to the One who embodied perfect holiness. The religious leaders often struggled to understand this reality because they expected separation where Jesus offered restoration. They anticipated immediate condemnation where He offered an opportunity for repentance. They measured people according to their past while He called them according to their future. The reader asks another question. “If Jesus welcomed sinners, does that mean truth no longer mattered?” “On the contrary. Truth mattered so deeply that He refused to leave anyone unchanged.” That distinction lies at the heart of this book. Truth is not the enemy of grace, and grace is not the absence of truth. The ministry of Jesus demonstrates that both can exist together without contradiction because both reveal the character of God. Truth uncovers what has been hidden, and grace provides what is necessary for transformation. Perhaps the greatest misunderstanding is not that people have rejected judgment but that they have mistaken judgment for the act of pointing at someone else. Scripture repeatedly directs the attention of the believer inward before it ever turns outward. The invitation is always to examine the heart, to repent, to be renewed, and to become more like Christ. The journey that follows is an invitation to return to that biblical pattern. It asks difficult questions, revisits familiar passages, and challenges assumptions that many of us have accepted without examination. Most importantly, it seeks to distinguish between the authority that belongs to God alone and the responsibility that belongs to every disciple who desires to walk in truth. If we are willing to make that journey together, we may discover that judgment is not what we imagined, that truth is far more beautiful than we believed, and that the God who called Adam from behind the trees is still calling humanity out of hiding and into His marvelous light.
