Chapter Nineteen

The People We Almost Miss

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 19 of 25

One of the most sobering realities in Scripture is how often God worked through people who could have been easily overlooked.

Where people see a finished story, God often sees a new beginning.

The longer I read the Bible, the more I notice that God's story is filled with individuals who would never have been the obvious choice. They were not always the strongest, the most qualified, the most respected, or the most likely candidates for the work God intended to accomplish. In many cases, they carried histories that would have caused others to dismiss them entirely.

Yet God saw something that others did not.

That observation becomes particularly important in light of everything we have explored throughout this book. If judgment often causes us to reduce people to a single moment, a single failure, or a single label, then redemption requires us to see people through a much larger lens. Redemption acknowledges the reality of a person's past while refusing to believe that the past possesses the authority to determine the future.

The ministry of Jesus demonstrates this repeatedly.

When most people looked at Matthew, they saw a tax collector. His profession alone was enough to shape public opinion. To many of his fellow Jews, he represented compromise, greed, and betrayal. Few people would have imagined that his story would one day become part of the foundation of the church.

Jesus saw something more.

He saw a disciple.

The invitation was remarkably simple.

"Follow Me."

With those words, a man known primarily for his profession began a journey that would forever change his life. The label that once defined him no longer had the final word.

The same pattern appears throughout the Gospels.

When others saw an impulsive fisherman, Jesus saw a future leader.

When others saw a dishonest tax collector hiding in a tree, Jesus saw a man whose heart was ready for repentance.

When others saw a broken woman arriving at a well in the heat of the day, Jesus saw someone who would carry news of the Messiah back to her community.

Again and again, Christ looked beyond the surface.

This does not mean He ignored reality. He knew people's failures better than anyone else. Nothing was hidden from Him. He understood their weaknesses, their motives, their fears, and their mistakes with perfect clarity. Yet His complete knowledge never prevented Him from seeing their potential.

That truth challenges the way we often view one another.

Human beings naturally categorize people. We create mental descriptions and often place individuals into those categories with surprising speed. Sometimes those categories are formed through personal experiences. Sometimes they emerge from rumors, assumptions, or first impressions. Once established, they can become remarkably difficult to change.

Jesus never allowed a person's past to become the limit of God's future.

The problem is not that observations are always wrong.

The problem is that observations are often incomplete.

No one knew this better than Jesus.

He understood that every person carries a story that extends beyond the chapter currently visible to everyone else. He understood that God's work within a human life is often unfolding beneath the surface long before outward evidence becomes apparent.

This reality should produce both humility and hope.

Humility reminds us that we rarely know as much as we think we do.

Hope reminds us that God is often accomplishing more than we can see.

As I reflected upon the people who appear throughout Scripture, I was struck by how many of them would have been dismissed if their stories had ended too early.

Abraham would have been remembered as an elderly man still waiting for a promise.

Jacob would have been remembered as a deceiver.

Moses would have been remembered as a fugitive hiding in the wilderness.

David would have been remembered for some of the darkest moments of his life.

Peter would have been remembered for his denial.

Paul would have been remembered for persecuting the church.

Yet God continued writing.

That simple truth may be one of the most encouraging realities found anywhere in Scripture.

God continued writing.

The chapter people saw was not the final chapter.

The failure people remembered was not the final word.

The weakness people observed was not the complete story.

Again and again, God demonstrated His ability to work beyond the limitations of human expectations.

Perhaps this is why judgment can be so dangerous when it hardens into certainty. It tempts us to believe that we understand the conclusion of a story that God is still writing. It encourages us to form permanent opinions about situations that remain unfinished.

The Lord does not share that limitation.

He sees beginnings and endings simultaneously.

He sees the wounds no one else understands.

He sees prayers that have not yet been answered.

He sees growth that remains invisible to everyone else.

Most importantly, He sees what His grace can accomplish within a willing heart.

The church should never forget this.

Grace sees what God is creating, not merely what people have been.

Every congregation contains people who are still becoming. Some are taking their first steps of faith. Others are recovering from failure. Some are carrying burdens they have never shared with another person. Others are quietly experiencing a work of God that has not yet become visible.

The temptation is to evaluate them according to where they are.

Jesus consistently viewed people according to where His grace could take them.

That perspective changes the way believers interact with one another. It encourages patience. It nurtures compassion. It creates space for growth. It reminds us that spiritual transformation is often a process rather than a single event.

Most importantly, it keeps us from missing people.

Throughout history, some of God's greatest works have begun in lives that others overlooked. The next leader, teacher, pastor, missionary, or faithful servant may currently be struggling, doubting, healing, or growing in ways that are not yet obvious.

The God who called fishermen, tax collectors, and former persecutors has not changed.

He is still finding people others underestimate.

He is still restoring people others have written off.

He is still calling people others almost miss.

As these reflections continued to settle within me, I realized that this truth reaches beyond the way we view others. It also touches the way we view ourselves.

For there are moments when the person we are most tempted to dismiss is the one staring back at us from the mirror.

And if God specializes in seeing beyond unfinished chapters, then perhaps we need to learn how to trust His view of our story as much as we trust His view of everyone else's.
