Chapter Twelve

Put Down the Stone

Estimated Reading Time • 10 Minutes

Chapter 12 of 25

Every journey eventually arrives at a moment when information must become action.

Throughout these chapters, we have explored questions that reach into some of the deepest places of the human heart. We have walked through the garden where blame first appeared, examined the temptation to occupy a throne that belongs to God alone, considered the difference between conviction and condemnation, and followed the ministry of Jesus as He consistently revealed a better way. We have stood in the temple courtyard with a woman caught in adultery, watched stones fall from the hands of her accusers, and followed Christ all the way to the cross where truth, justice, mercy, and grace met in perfect harmony.

Every truth we have discovered leads to one final decision.

The purpose of this journey was never simply to gain knowledge.

Knowledge alone rarely changes people.

Transformation occurs when truth is embraced deeply enough to reshape the way we live.

That is why the final question of this book is not primarily theological.

It is personal.

What will we do with the stones we have been carrying?

For some readers, those stones may take the form of opinions that have hardened over time. For others, they may be judgments formed years ago that continue to influence the way certain people are viewed. Some stones are made of resentment. Others are shaped by pride, disappointment, bitterness, or wounds that have never fully healed.

Whatever form they take, the result is often the same.

Stones create distance.

They separate people from one another.

They make restoration more difficult.

They encourage conclusions where understanding is needed.

Most importantly, they distract us from our own need for grace.

One of the most striking realities in Scripture is how often God directs His people inward before directing them outward. David prayed for God to search his heart. Isaiah confronted his own unworthiness before speaking to the nation. Peter's transformation began when he recognized his own need for mercy. Again and again, genuine spiritual growth emerged from humility rather than superiority.

This pattern stands in direct contrast to the instincts that often dominate human relationships.

We naturally notice the flaws of others more quickly than our own. We evaluate external actions while remaining unaware of hidden motives within ourselves. We are often far more patient with our personal failures than with the failures of those around them.

The teachings of Jesus challenge that tendency at every turn.

He consistently redirected attention toward the condition of the heart. His goal was not merely to modify behavior but to transform the person beneath the behavior. The change He sought was deeper than appearances and more lasting than temporary compliance.

That same invitation remains before us today.

The question is not whether other people need grace.

They do.

The question is whether we recognize how desperately we need it ourselves.

The answer to that question shapes everything else.

A person who remains aware of his own dependence upon God's mercy approaches others differently. Compassion becomes easier. Patience grows. Humility replaces self righteousness. Conversations begin to reflect the heart of Christ rather than the desire to prove a point.

The closer we draw to Christ, the less interested we become in condemning others.

This does not mean truth becomes less important.

In many ways, truth becomes even more important because it is no longer being used to elevate ourselves above others. Instead, it becomes the means through which God continues transforming us into the image of His Son.

The mature believer does not abandon truth.

The mature believer learns to carry it with humility.

That distinction may be one of the most important lessons in the entire Christian life.

As I have reflected on the themes explored throughout this book, I keep returning to the image of the temple courtyard. The woman stood before Jesus expecting judgment. The men surrounding her arrived confident in their conclusions. Yet by the end of the encounter, the stones had fallen, the accusers had departed, and mercy had accomplished what condemnation never could.

The story endures because it speaks to every generation.

We all know what it feels like to stand where the woman stood.

We know what it means to need forgiveness.

We know what it means to hope for mercy.

Yet many of us also know what it feels like to stand among the crowd.

We know the temptation to reach conclusions too quickly.

We know the temptation to focus on another person's failures while overlooking our own.

We know the temptation to carry stones.

The invitation of Christ remains the same.

Put them down.

Not because truth does not matter.

Not because holiness is unimportant.

Not because sin should be ignored.

Put them down because the role of ultimate Judge was never ours to begin with.

Put them down because mercy has been extended to us.

Put them down because grace accomplished in our lives what judgment alone never could.

Put them down because every person we encounter is someone for whom Christ died.

The world offers countless opportunities to condemn. Every day presents new reasons to become cynical, critical, or self righteous. Yet the follower of Jesus is called to something different. We are called to reflect the character of the One who saved us.

That calling requires courage.

It requires humility.

It requires a willingness to see people through the lens of redemption rather than merely through the lens of failure.

Most of all, it requires remembering the cross.

Lay down your stone. Take up your cross. Follow Jesus.

At Calvary, the One who possessed every right to judge chose instead to bear judgment. The One who knew every sin carried every burden. The One who could have condemned opened the door to reconciliation.

If we truly believe that, it becomes difficult to approach others with anything less than gratitude, humility, and compassion.

The title of this book began with a question.

Who made you judge?

After walking through Scripture together, perhaps the answer is clearer than it was at the beginning.

No one did.

The throne belongs to God.

The cross belongs to Christ.

The work of conviction belongs to the Spirit.

Our calling is different.

We are called to walk humbly, love mercy, pursue truth, and reflect the character of Jesus in a world that desperately needs Him.

If these pages have accomplished anything, my hope is not that they have provided new arguments. My hope is that they have encouraged deeper reflection. My hope is that they have led readers back to Scripture and, more importantly, back to the Savior revealed within its pages.

The journey ends where it began.

Standing before truth.

Standing before grace.

Standing before the One who knows us completely and loves us still.

And there, with empty hands and a humble heart, we finally discover what it means to put down the stone.

End of Chapter Twelve
